02

Disruptive pricing, market by market

Every one of these incumbents hides the price or charges in a way that grows their fee as the client's cost grows. The disruption is the same in each: make it transparent and align the fee to delivered savings.

401(k) advisory #1

Incumbent: 0.2–0.5% of plan assets, asset-based, conflicted
Disruption: flat annual fiduciary fee (3(38)), decoupled from assets
Asset-based fees punish the client as their balance grows and reward the advisor for doing nothing. A flat fee (for example $12k–$40k/yr by plan size) is a hard transparency wedge, and the bigger and more overpaying the plan, the more obvious the savings. Renata flags the high-fee plans; the flat fee closes them.

Property-tax appeals

Incumbent: ~50% contingency of year-one savings
Disruption: 30–40% contingency, or flat per-property, AI-evidenced
The 50% no-win-no-fee model is standard and sticky. Automated comp-pulling and evidence generation lets us undercut to 30–40% (or a flat fee per parcel) and still profit on volume. The pitch stays "you pay nothing unless we cut your bill," just cheaper and faster.

Employee benefits (core)

Incumbent: hidden commission baked into premium
Disruption: disclosed fee, paid on delivered savings
Higher premium means a higher hidden commission, so the incumbent has no reason to lower your cost. We disclose exactly what we make and tie it to the savings we find. The free audit is the top of the funnel.

Commercial P&C

Incumbent: embedded commission, 10–15% of premium
Disruption: flat or fee-based, transparent broker of record
Same transparency play as benefits, larger premiums, but the trigger data is messier (Form 5500 only covers insured benefits, not workers' comp or property). Strong economics, harder data. A later arm, not the wedge.

CRE lease renewals

Incumbent: landlord-paid commission (the conflict)
Disruption: tenant-side, rebate or flat fee
The tenant rep is usually paid by the landlord, which is the conflict. A transparent tenant-only model that rebates part of the commission is the disruption. But the lease-expiry trigger data is proprietary and gated, so our free-data edge does not transfer. Park it unless we buy data.

Govcon recompetes

Incumbent: expensive capture consultants, day rates
Disruption: success fee on awards, or AI capture intel
Recompete dates are a clean trigger, but SAM.gov bans UI scraping (sanctioned API only), and the operator path means becoming a bid shop, a different business. Interesting, compliance-boxed, lowest fit.
03

Where the math points

The recommendation

401(k) advisory is the cheapest re-skin with real economics, off the file we already ingest.

It is the same Form 5500 dataset, just different schedules, so nearly the whole ingestion, scoring, outbound, and CRM stack transfers in about a week. A ~$1.5–3B mid-market SAM, recurring fees of $15k–$80k per plan, and a flat-fee transparency wedge against asset-based incumbents. Property-tax appeals is the strongest license-free second move (pure contingency, public rolls, huge in Texas). The deeper point for Andrew and for investors: the engine is the asset, and the data is nearly free across a whole family of verticals. One 5500 pipeline can run a benefits brokerage, a 401(k) advisory, and a commercial P&C arm, with a property-tax contingency play on the side.

Caveats and honesty. Numbers are directional estimates, not audited. Form 5500 is a lagging signal (filed roughly 7 months after plan-year end), so it surfaces who is overpaying or who already switched, not a live, real-time moment; the durable edge is mispricing-versus-peers plus renewal timing, not switch prediction. The viral "80% of 401(k) plans overpay" stat was refuted in our research and is not used here. Commercial P&C and CRE re-skins do not run cleanly off Form 5500, so their data cost is higher than the headline fit suggests. SAM and beachhead figures are our assumptions about a winnable slice, and the beta exists to test the trigger's real conversion before scaling spend.
