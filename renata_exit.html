Renata · Exit, Acquirers & Comps

Who buys, at what multiple, and how fast.

The operator playbook only works if there is a buyer at the end of it. There is, and it is one of the most active M&A machines in finance. This is the exit map: the comps, the named acquirers, and the honest timeline to a sellable book.

01

The verdict

Fastest, highest-multiple exit

Build a recurring book in benefits + 401(k), then sell it into the insurance roll-up machine.

Established agency and advisory books sell at ~11–12x EBITDA on average (platform firms 14–18x with earnouts), and PE-backed aggregators drove ~71% of 854 US brokerage deals in 2025. The buyer demand is deep, durable, and serial. The 401(k) / retirement-plan advisory re-skin sits in the same machine, runs off the same Form 5500 file, and is consolidating fast, with proven roll-up value creation (CAPTRUST roughly tripled to $3.7B in three years across 29 acquisitions).

The hard caveat: those multiples are for established books with roughly $1M+ of durable recurring EBITDA. A brand-new two-founder shop has no sellable EBITDA for years. Realistically it is 3 to 5+ years of book-building before you are a tuck-in anyone will buy. There is no quick flip. The engine builds the book faster; it does not skip the calendar.

03

Who the acquirers are

Serial, PE-backed, and hungry. These are concrete exit counterparties, not theory.

401(k) / retirement advisory our pick

CAPTRUST (marquee roll-up) · OneDigital Retirement · Creative Planning · Hub Retirement · Mariner · SageView
Buying retirement books for purchasing power with recordkeepers and expanded fiduciary consulting. CAPTRUST alone did 29 deals in three years. Same Form 5500 file we already run.

Benefits & commercial P&C

BroadStreet Partners (#1, ~65–69 deals/yr) · Inszone (~45) · ALKEME (~42) · Hub International (~49) · Gallagher · Marsh McLennan Agency · Aon/NFP · OneDigital · AssuredPartners · World · Patriot Growth · Acrisure
The deepest tuck-in market in finance. The big strategics (Aon/NFP, MMA, OneDigital) buy benefits AND retirement, so a multi-line book off one engine is a more attractive target.

Property-tax appeals

Ryan LLC (the consolidator) · Altus Group · Kroll
Active buyer (3 property-tax acquisitions in 2022), but no public EBITDA-multiple comp surfaced, so liquidity and pricing rank below insurance and RPA. A cash business more than an exit story.

Govcon & CRE

Govcon: large primes (CACI, Booz Allen, Leidos, SAIC). CRE: CBRE, JLL, Cushman, Newmark, Colliers
Govcon multiples are great but only at large-cap scale, a steep discount for a small private firm. CRE majors were named but not verified as buyers of small data-driven operators. Both rank low for us.
04

The playbook to a sellable book

There is no shortcut to the multiple. The path is to build a recurring book faster than a normal agency could, across two lines the same buyers want, then tuck in.

01 · now
Become the operatorLicense, sign a GA for appointments and back-office, point Renata at Austin mid-market. Benefits first, 401(k) off the same file.
02 · yr 1–2
Build recurring revenueWin and retain accounts. Recurring commission and advisory fees are the asset. Retention and low client concentration are what a buyer diligences.
03 · yr 3–5
Reach the tuck-in floorRoughly $1M+ of durable EBITDA (a few $M revenue). Multi-line (benefits + retirement) makes you a cleaner target.
04 · exit
Sell into the machineTuck-in at ~8–12x EBITDA to a serial acquirer (CAPTRUST, OneDigital, BroadStreet, Inszone). Platform-level 14–18x comes only with scale.
Caveats and honesty. Every high multiple here is for an established book with durable recurring EBITDA, not a nascent two-founder shop. A new operator has no sellable EBITDA for years; tuck-ins trade at the low end (7.5–12x), platform deals (14–18x) require scale. Multiples are at cyclical highs (peaked Q3 2024, roughly flat through 2025) and are rate and credit-spread sensitive, so they could compress inside a 3 to 5 year window. M&A-advisory sources (Sica Fletcher, MarshBerry) are interested parties, though their figures are conservative and corroborated; deal-count rankings vary by provider. No multiple comps surfaced for the insurtech-native exits (Newfront, Nava). Open question: whether a proprietary Form 5500 data engine adds standalone acquisition value or a multiple premium, or whether acquirers only pay for the resulting book. The honest base case: they pay for the book; the engine's job is to build it faster.
